      At the beginning I should note that I registered in April so I didn't get involved in the guesswork in March. It has now become certain to me that pre-Easter Viddles are also important in connection with the investigation. This is reassuring in part because the current video has been released to the public following the notorious announcement expected on March 25th, so the Preacher has redesigned it and the Viddle presumably has up-to-date information in this Viddle.

       

      I had a theory that the names Rebus and Brodie have something to do with Detective Rebus and Jean Brodie but that's not the case now with the new name appearing, Dalhousie.

       

      I would like to take this opportunity to highlight a presumably important name: Ramsay

       

      • James Broun-Ramsay, the first Marquess (hehe) of Dalhousie. The person of interest mentioned here was a Scottish statesman and colonial administrator who was born in Scotland.
      • Clan Ramsay is a Lowland Scottish clan. By the 13rd century, this group of interest had five major branches and one of them was the Ramsays of Dalhousie.
      • Gordon Ramsay himself doesn't require too much explanation of who he is. He was born in Scotland. Once he related that one of his fondest memories is playing alongside one of Scotland's football legends, Ally McCoist. That's okay, not so much here. But this guy, Ally McCoist returned from an injury by coming off the bench to score an overhead kick to win the 1993 League Cup final against Hibernian*.

      *Remember Hibernian? The string of numbers (533814) has a meaning from our previous findings: if you type these numbers into the calculator and then turn it upside down, you can clearly read the word HIBEES. HIBEES is the nickname of the Scottish football team, Hibernian FC.

       

      Based on the aforementioned findings, it can be concluded that the term Dalhousie can be associated with the name Ramsay and Scotland. That's all for now.

      I became aware of another self-explanatory thing. After all, the article mentioned in my post above might make sense. I mentioned earlier that there is a connection between Rebus and Brodie, as both names appear among the literary gems of Scotland. Now, however, another name has been added to the investigation (Dalhousie), which may call into question the relationship I and the article assumed between the two names. However, as I explain the followings, perhaps the three names at once still have some significance.

       

      • Quick recap 1: Inspector Rebus, the protagonist of the series of novels by a Scottish author
      • Quick recap 2: Jean Brodie, the fictional character in a Scottish novel
      • And then Dalhousie: Miss Isabel Dalhousie appears as the main character in The Sunday Philosophy Club, a Scottish-based series of novels. The series is set in Edinburgh.

      In addition to the fact that the writings are clearly from Scots and all take place in Scotland, there is another important point in common: all three fictional character names are closely related to Scottish literature. In addition, the genre of all three series of novels is crime. I was just looking for an article that deals with those three names at once and I finally found an article by The Herald, a Scotland-based news media outlet.

      https://www.heraldscotland.com/news/12497889.step-aside-rebus-miss-dalhousie-is-about-to-join-the-ranks-of-edinburgh-sleuths/

      There aren’t many interesting things in the article, just a confirmation that the three fictional characters have something to do with each other: protagonists in Scottish crime novels.

       

      But here is a thing which may be more interesting than it seems at first glance: The story which involves Dalhouise is centred on the death of a young man who falls from the gods of the Usher Hall (interesting, right?) at the end of a symphony concert (the first Easter Viddle featured an outside event on which a symphonic background performance was playing with the lightshow, guess where, at Usher Hall).

      Another possible reference has occurred to my mind regarding Dalhousie.       The Acid House is a film adaptation of 3 short stories from Scottish author Irvine Welsh (he also wrote Trainspotting so he's well-known).

       

      One of the short stories in the film, according to wiki: "The Granton Star Cause": A comedy, in which Boab is having a rotten day. His parents throw him out so they can indulge in sado-masochism and he is sacked from his job, dumped by his girlfriend, and dropped from his football team. It has elements of Franz Kafka's The Metamorphosis. One of the characters is a pitiless, profane God who transforms him into a fly as punishment for wasting his life."

       

      This pitiless profane God sounds like a more vulgar version of Pastor Jim Standridge, linked in the summary, who was complaining "because if you loved me and you submitted to me, you'd know what my heart is and what my message is and you wouldn't go about establishing your own kingdom in the video room." Basically calling out camera guy for wasting his life as well. 

       

      Church of Skiatook, where the pastor was complaining, is in Ohio, and so is the Columbus Art Museum, for whom the Tumbleweeds video was commissioned for.

       

      In the video there's Monty Python theme and a music video with a white rabbit. Preacher has already shown a Monty Python sketch in another video, with the killer white rabbit. The pig squealing at the end is from Manhunt, and in the plot of Manhunt "Cash is instructed to follow the "White Rabbit", a man dressed in a rabbit costume, until the Director ultimately betrays him for the "final scene" of his film."

       

      The video was originally posted on 31st March, and quoted bible verses from Daniel.
